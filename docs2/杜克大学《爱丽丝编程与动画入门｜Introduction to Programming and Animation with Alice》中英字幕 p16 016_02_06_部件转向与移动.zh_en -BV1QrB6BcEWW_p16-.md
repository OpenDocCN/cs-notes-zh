In this session we'll explore turning versus moving when it comes to object parts， in short。

 you will wish to use Turner role rather than move when using instructions involving an object's parts。

We start this Alice project with two objects， a bunny and a child。

 Let's first look at what happens when we have the bunny turn its shoulder to the left and then to the right。

 As expected， the bunny swings its arm back and forth。Now。

 let's see what happens when we ask the bunny to move its shoulder the left and then back to the right。

 because the bunny's left shoulder is attached to the rest of the bunny。

 it cannot detach its shoulder。 Rather， Alice attempts to stretch the bunny's shoulder to the left。

 creating a very strange and awkward motion， one， which we wouldn't be unlikely to want the bunny to do。

Next， the Alice project continues code for the child to turn its knee forward and then backward。

 turning the knee forward is the normal sort of rotation we'd expect of the knee joint。Next。

 we have the child move its knee forward。 This is not an appropriate animation we'd like to do。

 Since the knee is attached， Alice cannot move the knee。 Rather， it stretches the knee forward。

 which is downward for the knee， causing a very strange animation， indeed。In the third case。

 we have the child turn its elbow。 This is the correct sort of rotation for the child's elbow。

 The child swings its arm back and forth。Lastly， we look at the effect of trying to move the child's elbow。

 Alice cannot detach the child's elbow from the rest of its body。

 Attempting to move the elbow results in a rather strange stretching of the elbow。

 Not what we likely wanted or intended。This concludes this short demonstration of moving versus turning or rolling of body parts。

 We almost never want to move body parts， or more generally object parts。 Instead。

 we want to turn or roll them depending on the orientation of the part to be rotated。
