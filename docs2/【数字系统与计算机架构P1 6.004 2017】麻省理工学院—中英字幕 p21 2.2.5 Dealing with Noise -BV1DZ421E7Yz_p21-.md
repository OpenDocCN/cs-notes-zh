There's one more issue we need to deal with before finalizing our signaling specification。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_2.png)

Consider the following combinational system where the upstream combinational device on the left is trying to send a digital zero to the downstream combinational device on the right。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_4.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_5.png)

The upstream device is generating an output voltage just slightly below V sub L， which。

 according to our proposed signaling specification， qualifies as the representation for a digital0。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_7.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_8.png)

Now suppose some electrical noise slightly changes the voltage on the wire so that the voltage detected on the input of the downstream device is slightly above Viabel。

 In other words， the receive signal no longer qualifies as a valid digital input。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_10.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_11.png)

And the combinational behavior of the downstream device is no longer guaranteed。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_13.png)

Oops， our system is behaving incorrectly because of some small amount of electrical noise。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_15.png)

Just the sort of flaky behavior we are hoping to avoid by adopting a digital systems architecture。

One way to address the problem is to adjust the signaling specification so that outputs have to obey tighter bounds than the inputs。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_17.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_18.png)

The idea being to ensure that valid output signals can be affected by noise without becoming invalid input signals。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_20.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_21.png)

Can we avoid the problem altogether by somehow avoiding noise？



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_23.png)

A nice thought， but not a goal that we can achieve if we're planning to use electrical components。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_25.png)

Voltage noise， which will define as variations away from nominal voltage values。

 comes from a variety of sources。

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_27.png)

Noise can be caused by electrical effects， such as IR drops in conductors due to Ohm's Law。

 Capitive coupling between conductors。

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_29.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_30.png)

And LDIDT effects caused by inductance in the components leads and changing currents。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_32.png)

Voltage deviations can be caused by manufacturing variations and component parameters from their nominal values that lead to small differences in electrical behavior。

 device to device。

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_34.png)

Voltage can be affected by environmental factors， such as thermal noise or voltage effects from external electromagnetic fields。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_36.png)

The list goes on。Note that in many cases， noise is caused by normal operation of the circuit or is an inherent property of the materials and processes used to make the circuits。

 and so is unavoidable。

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_38.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_39.png)

However， we can predict the magnitude of the noise and adjust our signaling specifications appropriately。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_41.png)

Let's see how this would work。

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_43.png)

Our proposed fix to the noise problem is to provide separate signaling specification for digital inputs and digital outputs。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_45.png)

To send to zero， digital outputs must produce a voltage less than or equal to VOL。

And tocentta1 produce a voltage greater than or equal to VOH。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_47.png)

So far， this doesn't seem very different than our previous signaling specification。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_49.png)

The difference is that digital inputs must obey a different signaling specification。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_51.png)

Input voltages less than or equal to VL must be interpreted as a digital0。

 and input voltages greater than or equal to VIH must be interpreted as a digital one。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_53.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_54.png)

The values of these four signaling thresholds are chosen to satisfy the constraints shown here。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_56.png)

Note that VIL is strictly greater than VOL， and VIH is strictly less than VOH。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_58.png)

The gaps between the input and output voltage thresholds are called the noise margins。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_60.png)

The noise margins tell us how much noise can be added to a valid 0 or a valid 1 output signal and still have the result interpreted correctly at the inputs to which it is connected。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_62.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_63.png)

The smaller of the two noise margins is called the noise immunity of the signaling specification。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_65.png)

Our goal as digital engineers is to design our signaling specifications to provide as much noise immunity as possible。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_67.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_68.png)

Combinational devices that obey the signaling specification work to remove the noise in their inputs before it has a chance to accumulate and eventually cause signaling errors。



![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_70.png)

![](img/44b41dfc5a500ed816bc80abe99b21b8_71.png)

The bottom line。Digital signaling doesn't suffer from the problems we saw in our earlier analog signaling example。
