Another form of differential pay is geographic pay， which relates to where workers live。

 The same rule may pay more or less， depending on where the worker is located。 In this video。

 we'll discuss geographic pay and provide an example as well。



![](img/12e707a40e816b4f2fcd7b2bf7104e21_2.png)

Most organizations that have employees in more than one location provide geographic pay differentials。

 This process accounts for economic differences between different locations。

 Some organizations base geographic pay on variations and the cost of living。 For example。

 paying employees more in high cost New York City than those in lower cost Kansas City。

 It is much more common， however， for organizations to base differential pay on the cost of labor。



![](img/12e707a40e816b4f2fcd7b2bf7104e21_4.png)

In the cost of labor approach， organizations use salary surveys to determine the prevailing salary levels for jobs in each location and adjust their pay scales accordingly。

 HR professionals can evaluate differences in cost of living by using the cost of living index provided by the council for community and economic research known as the C2 Er。

 This index allows users to set and compare consumer pricing trends across locations。

 The index reviews local customer prices for a common basket of 60 goods and services across more than 300 metropolitan areas。

 These comparisons can help an HR team determine a fair compensation for someone living in a comparatively less or more expensive place。
